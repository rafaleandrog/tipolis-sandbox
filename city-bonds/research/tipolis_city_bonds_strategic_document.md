# Tipolis City Bonds — Strategic Capital Markets Document
## Institutional Framework, Product Suite, and Investment Thesis

---

## 1. Context and Strategic Framing

Tipolis is building International Cities — jurisdictions designed from the ground up for the 21st century, operating under Special Administrative Region frameworks negotiated with host countries. Each International City is developed and operated through a Special Purpose Vehicle jointly created between Tipolis and the host country government. This SPV structure isolates the project financially and legally, allowing Tipolis to raise private capital without placing obligations on the sovereign balance sheet of the host country and without consolidating project-level debt onto Tipolis's own corporate balance sheet.

Within this structure, the core financing challenge is straightforward but consequential: International Cities require large volumes of capital, deployed with speed, at competitive cost, across long time horizons. Real estate development, hard infrastructure — ports, roads, railways, energy grids, water treatment — and urban infrastructure such as streets, lighting, and public spaces collectively demand a capital stack that conventional channels simply cannot supply efficiently. Equity partnerships are slow and expensive to structure. Government budget allocations are insufficient and politically constrained. Commercial bank debt is short-duration and unsuitable for the long investment horizon of city development.

City Bonds are Tipolis's answer to this structural problem. They are structured debt and equity instruments issued to raise private capital from institutional and qualified investors, with returns tied to the operational performance of city assets and structured exposure to Bitcoin appreciation as an asymmetric return component. The design draws directly from the capital markets innovation model established by Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy), which has demonstrated that a company can build a systematic program of issuing structured financial instruments — each calibrated to a specific investor risk appetite — to fund a long-term capital strategy anchored in a scarce, appreciating asset.

For Tipolis, that scarce asset is Bitcoin. The operational engine is the International City itself.

---

## 2. The City Bond as a Revenue Bond — Legal and Structural Analogy

For regulatory, legal, and investor communication purposes, City Bonds are most precisely analogous to municipal revenue bonds in the tradition of the United States and other developed capital markets. Revenue bonds are fixed-income instruments issued by a municipality or special authority — not backed by taxing power or the full faith and credit of a sovereign government — but instead secured by the revenues generated by a specific project or enterprise: toll roads, airports, water utilities, stadium facilities, convention centers.

The City Bond adopts exactly this structure. The underlying collateral is not the creditworthiness of Tipolis's corporate balance sheet nor any sovereign guarantee from the host country. It is the income-generating capacity of the assets funded by the bond proceeds: rental income from real estate, concession revenues from infrastructure, service fees from urban systems. The 9% cap rate assumption applied to the operational allocation reflects the projected stabilized yield on these city assets, analogous to a revenue coverage ratio in a traditional municipal revenue bond indenture.

The key innovations that City Bonds introduce beyond the standard revenue bond model are two:

First, the Bitcoin reserve component. Ten percent of every capital raise is allocated at deployment into Bitcoin, purchased on established liquid markets and held on Tipolis's balance sheet for the life of the instrument. This creates a reserve that appreciates independently of the city's operational performance and whose value at maturity — for the CB instruments — is partially distributed to investors as an asymmetric optionality component layered on top of the fixed coupon structure.

Second, the programmatic issuance architecture. Rather than one-off project-specific bonds, Tipolis intends to build a recurring capital markets program — analogous to an At-The-Market equity program but applied to debt and hybrid instruments — allowing continuous access to capital markets as project phases require funding. This programmatic approach reduces transaction costs, builds investor familiarity, and allows the credit profile to compound over time as successive issuances demonstrate repayment discipline.

## 3. SPV Architecture and Credit Structure

Each International City project is housed in an SPV jointly owned by Tipolis and the host country government. Capital raised through City Bonds flows into this SPV and is deployed according to the bond indenture's prescribed allocation. The SPV owns the city assets — land, buildings, infrastructure concessions — and the revenue streams from those assets service the bond obligations.

This structure provides several layers of credit support. The SPV's assets constitute real collateral against which senior bond tranches have direct claims. The operational revenues of the city provide the primary debt service coverage. Bitcoin reserves held by Tipolis serve as an additional collateral layer whose value is expected to appreciate over the bond's life. And the host country's co-ownership of the SPV creates implicit political alignment — the government has a financial interest in the city's success and thus in the bond's performance.

The objective of this credit architecture is to achieve investment-grade ratings from recognized international rating agencies — S&P, Moody's, or Fitch — for the senior tranche (CB6-20y). Achieving and improving this credit rating over time is a central strategic priority of the City Bond program, as it determines the cost of capital, the breadth of the investor universe, and Tipolis's ability to compete with conventional infrastructure financing alternatives.

## 4. Product Suite — Three Instruments

The City Bond suite consists of three instruments, each calibrated to a distinct position in the capital stack and a distinct investor risk-return profile. They are structurally analogous to three instruments in Strategy's capital markets architecture: STRF (senior, high-grade), STRD (subordinated, high-yield), and STRK (equity-like, perpetual preferred).

All three instruments share the following structural constants:

- **Operational allocation:** 90% of capital raised is deployed into city assets — real estate, hard infrastructure, or urban infrastructure as specified per issuance.
- **Bitcoin reserve allocation:** 10% of capital raised is deployed into Bitcoin on the date of issuance. This Bitcoin is 100% the property of Tipolis from the moment of purchase. Tipolis may use it as institutional collateral, sell it, lend it, or deploy it in any other strategic capacity it deems appropriate. The commitment to investors in the CB category (CB6-20y and CB10-10y) is not to hold those specific Bitcoin units unchanged — it is to deliver, at maturity, a cash amount equivalent to 50% of the Bitcoin appreciation calculated from the price at issuance to the price at maturity. The method by which Tipolis manages the underlying BTC position in the interim is at its sole discretion.
- **Cap rate assumption:** 9% per annum on the operational allocation, after a 3-year construction and ramp-up period during which no operational revenue is generated.
- **Ramp period:** Years 1 through 3 generate zero operational revenue. Monthly distribution obligations to investors begin from month 1 regardless, creating a temporary negative carry for the issuer during construction. This is by design and mirrors the economics of any long-duration infrastructure project.
- **Payment frequency:** All instruments — CB6-20y, CB10-10y, and CIP — make distributions to investors on a monthly basis.

### 4.0 Issuance Entity — Open Question Under Analysis

A critical structural decision that remains under active evaluation is the legal entity responsible for issuing each instrument. There are three possible configurations: issuance directly by Tipolis as the corporate parent, issuance by the project-level SPV, or a hybrid framework in which both entities may issue depending on the nature of the instrument and the project. Each option carries distinct advantages and disadvantages. This decision has not been finalized and will be subject to detailed legal, regulatory, and financial analysis before any issuance proceeds.

**Option A — Issuance directly by Tipolis:**

*Advantage 1:* Tipolis's corporate balance sheet consolidates the capital markets program, allowing investors to underwrite a single, diversified counterparty rather than evaluating each project SPV individually. This broadens the investor universe and reduces the due diligence burden per issuance, which accelerates deal timelines and lowers the effective cost of capital at the program level.

*Advantage 2:* A unified Tipolis issuance program builds a single, compounding credit track record. Each repaid coupon improves the same credit profile, creating a consistent and accumulating signal of repayment discipline that benefits all future issuances across all projects.

*Disadvantage 1:* Issuance at the Tipolis corporate level consolidates project-level liabilities onto the parent balance sheet. A project underperformance or operational default in one International City may affect Tipolis's overall credit standing and potentially constrain its ability to raise capital for other projects.

*Disadvantage 2:* Corporate-level issuance may trigger more stringent regulatory requirements across multiple jurisdictions — particularly for distribution to institutional investors in the United States and Europe — increasing compliance costs and legal complexity relative to an SPV-level structure, which may benefit from project-finance treatment under applicable securities regulations.

**Option B — Issuance by the project SPV:**

*Advantage 1:* SPV-level issuance provides structural ring-fencing. Each city project stands on its own financial merits. A difficulty in one International City does not legally contaminate the obligations of another, protecting investors in performing projects and limiting contagion risk across the broader Tipolis portfolio.

*Advantage 2:* Revenue bond treatment at the SPV level is a well-understood legal and regulatory category in most institutional investor jurisdictions. SPV-issued instruments secured by project revenues may be more readily classified and purchased by pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds operating under defined investment mandates.

*Disadvantage 1:* Each SPV issuance is a standalone credit assessment. Without a consolidated Tipolis track record, early SPVs face cold-start credit risk — investors must evaluate a new entity with no payment history, likely resulting in higher initial yields and smaller issuance volumes until a project-specific track record is established.

*Disadvantage 2:* Managing multiple independent capital markets programs across multiple SPVs increases operational complexity significantly. Investor relations, covenant monitoring, regulatory reporting, and monthly distribution servicing must be maintained separately for each SPV, multiplying compliance costs and management overhead.

**Option C — Hybrid framework (both entities may issue):**

This option is also under consideration and may represent the most flexible long-term architecture: CB6-20y and CB10-10y issued at the SPV level for project-specific financing with revenue bond treatment, while CIP is issued at the Tipolis corporate level given its perpetual preferred equity nature and its role in building Tipolis's aggregate Bitcoin reserve. No final determination has been made. The pros and cons of the hybrid approach will be evaluated alongside Options A and B in the formal legal and financial structuring process.

### 4.1 CB6-20y — City Bond Senior, 6%, 20 Years (STRF Equivalent)

**Classification:** High Grade, Senior Secured

**Structure:** CB6-20y is a 20-year fixed-rate senior secured bond paying a coupon of 6% per annum on the full principal amount. Coupon payments are distributed monthly. At the end of year 20, the investor receives: (i) the final monthly coupon installment; (ii) the full return of principal; and (iii) an amount in USD equivalent to 50% of the appreciation of Bitcoin calculated from the reference BTC price at issuance to the BTC price at maturity, applied to a notional Bitcoin allocation equal to 10% of the bond's face value.

**Financial mechanics (per $1,000,000 invested):**

The investor deploys $1,000,000. The issuing entity allocates $900,000 to operational city assets — generating, once stabilized, $81,000 per year ($6,750 per month) at a 9% cap rate — and deploys $100,000 into Bitcoin on day one. That Bitcoin is wholly owned by Tipolis and may be managed strategically throughout the bond's life. The contractual obligation to the investor is to pay, at maturity, the USD equivalent of 50% of the appreciation from the BTC issuance price to the BTC maturity price on the notional $100,000 allocation. The investor receives $60,000 per year — $5,000 per month — every month for 20 years ($1,200,000 in total coupons). At year 20, the investor also receives $1,000,000 principal plus the BTC appreciation payment described above.

Under the base case scenario of 29% Bitcoin CAGR over 20 years — consistent with Bitcoin's historical long-term compounding rate — the $100,000 notional BTC position compounds to approximately $16,285,000 (1.29^20 ≈ 162.85). The investor receives 50% of the $16,185,000 appreciation, or approximately $8,092,500, in addition to the $1,200,000 in coupons already received and the $1,000,000 principal. Total cash received by the investor: approximately $10,292,500 against a $1,000,000 original investment. Investor IRR in this base scenario: approximately 14.5%.

In a conservative scenario of 12% Bitcoin CAGR over 20 years, the $100,000 BTC notional grows to approximately $964,600 (1.12^20 ≈ 9.646). The investor receives 50% of the $864,600 appreciation, or approximately $432,300. Total investor receipts: $2,632,300. Investor IRR: approximately 7.1%.

In a bear case where Bitcoin does not appreciate (0% CAGR), the investor receives only the coupons and principal — $2,200,000 total — with an IRR equal to the coupon rate of 6.0%. The instrument performs as a plain-vanilla senior bond even without any Bitcoin appreciation.

**Tipolis carry:** During the 3-year ramp, the monthly distribution obligation of $5,000 per bond is unmatched by operational revenue. Maximum ramp-period carry deficit per bond: $180,000 (36 months × $5,000). From month 37 onward, operational revenue is $6,750/month while the distribution obligation is $5,000/month, generating positive carry of +$1,750 per bond per month (+$21,000/year). Over years 4 through 20 (17 years), Tipolis accumulates $357,000 in positive carry per bond from operational income alone, before any Bitcoin consideration.

**Debt service coverage ratio:** 1.35× (stabilized operational revenue of $81,000/year versus annual coupon obligation of $60,000).

**Duration:** Macaulay duration of approximately 12.2 years. Modified duration approximately 11.4 years.

**Capital deployment — eligible projects:** All Tipolis International City projects. Real estate (residential, commercial, hospitality), hard infrastructure (energy, water, roads, ports, railways), and urban infrastructure (streets, lighting, public spaces). The 20-year duration aligns particularly well with long-lived hard infrastructure concessions.

**Investor return scenarios — per $1,000,000 invested:**

| BTC CAGR | Total monthly coupons (20y) | 50% BTC gain (USD, at maturity) | Total investor gain | Investor IRR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0% (bear) | $1,200,000 | $0 | $1,200,000 | 6.0% |
| 12% (conservative) | $1,200,000 | $432,300 | $1,632,300 | ~7.1% |
| 29% (base) | $1,200,000 | $8,092,500 | $9,292,500 | ~14.5% |
| 40% (bull) | $1,200,000 | $41,784,000 | $42,984,000 | ~22% |

Note: The 40% CAGR bull case figure reflects $100,000 × (1.40^20 − 1) × 50% ≈ $41,784,000. At these appreciation levels the Bitcoin component overwhelmingly dominates total return. Even at the conservative 12% CAGR, the instrument returns significantly more than a plain senior bond.

**Target investor profiles:**

Sovereign wealth funds are the natural anchor investors for CB6-20y. Their mandate requires long-duration real assets with predictable income and capital preservation. The senior secured position satisfies investment policy requirements; the 20-year duration matches their liability-free perpetual capital; the Bitcoin optionality is additive without compromising the credit investment thesis.

Pension funds and life insurance companies are the second most natural investor class, using CB6-20y for asset-liability management. A 20-year duration bond with 1.35× debt service coverage and real asset backing is structurally consistent with how these institutions match long-tail liabilities. The monthly distribution schedule aligns with standard institutional income management.

Infrastructure funds seeking senior tranche exposure — particularly those operating with defined investment-grade mandates — are appropriate investors. The coverage ratio, senior secured status, and real asset collateral are the three criteria that most infrastructure credit committees require.

Conservative family offices with a preference for long-duration real assets and diversification away from conventional equity-bond portfolios will find CB6-20y attractive as a yield-generating instrument with embedded Bitcoin exposure at no additional cost.

**Investor pitch:** You are purchasing a senior secured instrument backed by city-scale real assets generating 1.35 times your annual coupon obligation, paid monthly. Your 6% annual income is contractually fixed and received every month for 20 years. At maturity, your full principal is returned. Additionally, the USD equivalent of 50% of Bitcoin's appreciation over 20 years is delivered to you at no additional cost — with no Bitcoin custody responsibility during the holding period. Even if Bitcoin does not appreciate at all, you hold a senior bond paying 6% with real asset backing. The Bitcoin participation is asymmetric optionality embedded in a high-grade credit instrument.

### 4.2 CB10-10y — City Bond Subordinated, 10%, 10 Years (STRD Equivalent)

**Classification:** High Yield, Subordinated

**Structure:** CB10-10y is a 10-year subordinated bond paying a coupon of 10% per annum on the full principal amount. Coupon payments are distributed monthly. At the end of year 10, the investor receives: (i) the final monthly coupon installment; (ii) the full return of principal; and (iii) an amount in USD equivalent to 50% of the appreciation of Bitcoin calculated from the reference BTC price at issuance to the BTC price at maturity, applied to a notional Bitcoin allocation equal to 10% of the bond's face value. As with CB6-20y, the Bitcoin deployed at issuance is wholly owned and managed by Tipolis. The investor's right is to a cash settlement at maturity, not to any specific Bitcoin units.

**Financial mechanics (per $1,000,000 invested):**

The investor deploys $1,000,000. The issuing entity allocates $900,000 to operational city assets — generating $81,000/year ($6,750/month) at a 9% cap rate once stabilized — and deploys $100,000 into Bitcoin on day one, retained as Tipolis corporate property. The investor receives $100,000 per year — $8,333 per month — for 10 years ($1,000,000 in total coupons). At year 10, the investor receives $1,000,000 principal plus the BTC appreciation payment.

Under the base case of 29% Bitcoin CAGR over 10 years, the $100,000 notional BTC grows to approximately $1,275,700 (1.29^10 ≈ 12.757). The investor receives 50% of the $1,175,700 appreciation, or approximately $587,850, in addition to $1,000,000 in coupons and $1,000,000 in principal. Total investor receipts: approximately $2,587,850. Investor IRR: approximately 13.2%.

In a conservative 12% CAGR scenario, the $100,000 BTC grows to approximately $310,600 (1.12^10 ≈ 3.106). The investor receives 50% of the $210,600 appreciation, or approximately $105,300. Total investor receipts: $2,105,300. Investor IRR: approximately 10.5%.

In a bear case with 0% Bitcoin appreciation, the investor receives only coupons and principal — $2,000,000 total — with an IRR equal to the coupon rate of 10.0%.

**Tipolis carry:** During the 3-year ramp, the monthly distribution obligation of $8,333 per bond is fully unmatched by operational revenue, creating a deficit of $8,333/month per bond. Maximum ramp-period deficit per bond: $300,000 (36 months × $8,333). From month 37 onward, operational revenue of $6,750/month is less than the distribution obligation of $8,333/month, resulting in a persistent negative carry of −$1,583 per bond per month (−$19,000/year). This negative carry is managed at the enterprise level by: (i) the BTC appreciation retained by Tipolis at maturity and beyond; (ii) positive carry generated by CB6-20y issuances in the same portfolio; and (iii) operational revenues from other city assets not pledged to specific bond issuances. The negative carry on CB10-10y is a deliberate product design — Tipolis accepts the temporary cost in exchange for portfolio diversification and access to high-yield capital markets.

**Debt service coverage ratio:** 0.81× in stabilized years. This is below 1.0× by design, consistent with subordinated high-yield instruments in traditional capital markets.

**Duration:** Macaulay duration of approximately 7.0 years. Modified duration approximately 6.4 years.

**Capital deployment — eligible projects:** All Tipolis International City projects. Given the 10-year term, deployment is most naturally suited to real estate assets and concession-based infrastructure with faster revenue maturation profiles.

**Investor return scenarios — per $1,000,000 invested:**

| BTC CAGR | Total monthly coupons (10y) | 50% BTC gain (USD, at maturity) | Total investor gain | Investor IRR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0% (bear) | $1,000,000 | $0 | $1,000,000 | 10.0% |
| 12% (conservative) | $1,000,000 | $105,300 | $1,105,300 | ~10.5% |
| 29% (base) | $1,000,000 | $587,850 | $1,587,850 | ~13.2% |
| 40% (bull) | $1,000,000 | $1,396,250 | $2,396,250 | ~16.2% |

Note: 40% CAGR bull case: $100,000 × (1.40^10 − 1) × 50% = $100,000 × 27.925 × 50% ≈ $1,396,250.

**Target investor profiles:**

High yield bond funds are the primary institutional target. CB10-10y pays 10% fixed with a defined 10-year maturity and real asset backing — a combination that traditional high-yield credit, which typically involves unsecured corporate obligations, cannot match on collateral quality.

Private credit funds represent a strong fit. The 10-year fixed maturity, subordinated claim with real asset collateral, and Bitcoin appreciation payment at maturity differentiate CB10-10y from standard private credit instruments, which rarely offer any asymmetric appreciation component at the back end of the investment.

Bitcoin conviction investors who want meaningful exposure to Bitcoin's long-term price trajectory without managing custody risk and while receiving 10% per year in monthly cash distributions. The 50% Bitcoin appreciation payment at year 10 is the most direct available mechanism to access Bitcoin's compounding in a regulated, fixed-income structure.

Endowments and foundations with 10-year investment horizons that seek monthly income while participating in Bitcoin's appreciation. Monthly distributions at 10% annualized support operational budgets while the BTC component contributes to long-term capital growth.

**Investor pitch:** You receive 10% annually in monthly payments from a real asset-backed instrument with a fixed 10-year maturity. At year 10, on top of your full principal, you receive the USD equivalent of 50% of Bitcoin's appreciation since issuance — without any custody responsibility. At the base case of 29% Bitcoin CAGR, your total IRR exceeds 13%. This is private equity-level return with monthly cash distributions and a contractual maturity date.

### 4.3 CIP — City Equity Participation (STRK Equivalent)

**Classification:** Equity Hybrid, Perpetual Preferred

**Structure:** CIP (City Equity Participation) is a perpetual preferred equity instrument with a guaranteed minimum annual distribution of 4% per annum as a floor, plus direct participation in the equity results of the real estate project the instrument finances. Distributions are paid monthly. It has no fixed maturity date and does not provide for the return of principal at any predetermined date. There is no BTC upside commitment to CIP investors — the Bitcoin purchased with the 10% allocation of CIP proceeds is 100% the property of Tipolis and accrues entirely to its balance sheet with no sharing or settlement obligation toward CIP investors at any point.

CIP is exclusively deployed into real estate for residential and commercial incorporation projects, plus directly associated urban infrastructure costs (streets, lighting, utilities serving the funded real estate). It does not finance hard infrastructure.

**Return structure — floor plus equity participation:**

The investor's total monthly distribution is the sum of two components: (i) a contractually guaranteed floor of 4% per annum on invested capital, distributed monthly; and (ii) a variable monthly distribution representing direct participation in the results of the equity project the tranche finances. The variable component has no ceiling. The three reference scenarios for the equity participation component are:

- **Pessimistic scenario:** +0% additional distribution above the floor. Total investor return: **4.0% per annum** ($3,333/month per $1,000,000 invested).
- **Base scenario:** +5% additional distribution above the floor. Total investor return: **9.0% per annum** ($7,500/month per $1,000,000 invested).
- **Optimistic scenario:** +12% additional distribution above the floor. Total investor return: **16.0% per annum** ($13,333/month per $1,000,000 invested).

These ranges are illustrative. Actual distributions above the floor will vary with the specific project's operational performance, occupancy rates, sales execution, and market conditions in each ICT. No distribution above the 4% floor is contractually guaranteed.

**Important notice — structural elements under active discussion:**

The following CIP parameter has not yet been finalized and will be resolved before any formal issuance:

**Exit mechanism:** The current working assumption is that the primary investor exit is through secondary market trading of CIP units. However, it has not yet been determined whether the instrument will include any optional redemption right for Tipolis, any put option for investors after a defined holding period, or any structured buyback program. Whether CIP will be exclusively negotiated on a secondary market or will incorporate issuer-side exit provisions is an open question that will be addressed in the formal legal structuring process.

Investors and advisors reviewing this document should treat the exit mechanism described in the return scenarios below as a working assumption for the purpose of product definition and investor dialogue — not as a final, binding term.

**Financial mechanics (per $1,000,000 invested — working assumptions):**

The issuing entity allocates $900,000 to real estate operations — targeting $81,000/year ($6,750/month) at a 9% cap rate once stabilized — and deploys $100,000 into Bitcoin on day one. That Bitcoin is wholly and permanently owned by Tipolis with no obligation to CIP investors. The investor receives a guaranteed minimum distribution of $3,333 per month ($40,000/year) regardless of project performance. When operational results exceed the floor, monthly distributions increase, with no ceiling on the upside.

**Tipolis carry at the floor:** Operational revenue of $6,750/month minus the minimum monthly distribution of $3,333 equals positive carry of +$3,417 per unit per month (+$41,000/year) from month 37 onward. This is the most favorable carry position for Tipolis of any instrument in the suite. During the 3-year ramp, Tipolis pays the $3,333/month floor distribution without operational revenue, creating a carry deficit of $3,333/month per unit during construction. Maximum ramp-period deficit: $120,000 (36 months × $3,333).

**Debt service coverage ratio at floor:** 2.03× (stabilized operational revenue of $81,000/year versus floor distribution of $40,000/year).

**Investor return scenarios — per $1,000,000 invested (10-year hold, exit at par):**

| Performance scenario | Equity participation | Total annual return | Monthly distribution | 10-year total distributions | IRR |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pessimistic | +0% | 4.0% | $3,333 | $400,000 | 4.0% |
| Base case | +5% | 9.0% | $7,500 | $900,000 | 9.0% |
| Optimistic | +12% | 16.0% | $13,333 | $1,600,000 | 16.0% |

Note: IRR assumes secondary market exit at par after 10 years, which is one of the exit options under evaluation. If the secondary market values CIP above par due to strong distribution performance, actual IRR exceeds these figures. No BTC component is reflected in any CIP investor return scenario.

**Tipolis Bitcoin accumulation (per $1,000,000 CIP issued):**

The $100,000 deployed into Bitcoin on day one is permanently retained on Tipolis's balance sheet and is never returned to or shared with CIP investors under any scenario. It compounds indefinitely as a Tipolis corporate asset. At 29% CAGR, the $100,000 position is worth approximately $1,275,700 after 10 years and approximately $16,285,000 after 20 years. Every CIP issuance adds to Tipolis's permanent Bitcoin reserve — this is the mechanism by which Tipolis systematically builds its strategic balance sheet position through the equity hybrid instrument.

**Capital deployment — real estate focus:**

CIP proceeds are exclusively allocated to residential and commercial real estate incorporation projects within International Cities, plus the directly associated urban infrastructure costs required to service those real estate assets. Hard infrastructure — ports, energy, roads, water — is not financed through CIP. The rationale is that real estate generates the most direct and measurable performance-linked revenue streams, making it appropriate for a performance-linked equity distribution instrument.

**Target investor profiles:**

Real estate equity funds are the most natural institutional match for CIP. The instrument mirrors the economic structure of direct property equity: monthly distributions tied to operational performance, no fixed maturity, exit through market mechanisms, and exposure to the city's long-term real estate appreciation. CIP removes the operational complexity of direct property ownership while preserving the economic exposure.

Strategic co-investors and partners — organizations aligned with the ICT thesis for reasons beyond pure financial return — find CIP attractive because the unlimited distribution upside creates genuine alignment with Tipolis's operational team and the city's overall development trajectory.

Local real estate developers who operate as contractors or development partners in the construction and delivery of ICT projects represent a particularly well-suited CIP investor segment. These firms are already embedded in the project's execution, understand the local market and asset quality, and may have a direct interest in capturing the long-term equity upside of the developments they help build. Offering CIP access to these partners creates financial alignment across the value chain — the developer who builds the city also participates in the returns it generates — while giving Tipolis a structured mechanism to deepen its relationships with key construction and development partners.

Alternative income and yield funds seeking perpetual income instruments with no reinvestment risk, no duration mismatch, and open-ended upside participation above a guaranteed floor. CIP provides something conventional preferred stock does not: real asset backing and a floor guarantee tied to a defined operational asset rather than unsecured corporate credit.

**Investor pitch:** You hold permanent capital in a real estate operation with a contractually guaranteed 4% floor — paid monthly — and direct equity participation in its performance, with no ceiling. You have no maturity date and no reinvestment risk. In the base scenario, you receive $7,500 per month per $1,000,000 invested. In the optimistic scenario, $13,333 per month. Your exit is through the secondary market at the time and price of your choosing. The floor is contractual. This is equity in a growing International City with permanent downside protection built into the structure.

## 6. Why Tipolis Must Build Its Own Financial Infrastructure

### 6.1 The Strategic Case for Vertical Integration in Capital Markets

The creation of the City Bond suite is a significant inflection point for Tipolis and its International City SPVs. But the product itself is not the primary strategic asset. The primary strategic asset is the infrastructure — regulatory, technological, compliance, origination, structuring, and distribution — that issues these products, connects with investors, and manages the ongoing capital markets program.

Any institution with sufficient legal expertise can create a bond instrument, define its terms, and present it to investors. The product is, in this sense, a commodity. What is not a commodity — and what constitutes a durable competitive advantage — is the distribution channel: the relationships with capital allocators, the investor database, the track record of execution, the regulatory licenses, the trust accumulated over successive issuances. This is the insight that has driven the most successful capital markets businesses in history: the institution that controls the relationship between capital and opportunity captures a structurally superior share of the economics.

For Tipolis, the practical implication is this: if City Bonds are distributed and serviced through third-party financial intermediaries — investment banks, placement agents, compliance service providers — Tipolis pays fees at every step of the process. Origination fees, structuring fees, distribution commissions, compliance outsourcing costs, and ongoing servicing fees collectively erode the economics of every issuance. More importantly, the investor relationship belongs to the intermediary. Tipolis does not develop the institutional knowledge of who its investors are, what they need, how they make decisions, and how to retain them for the next issuance.

Building proprietary financial infrastructure — legal entity structures with the appropriate regulatory licenses, a technology stack for issuance and compliance, a direct investor relations function, and an origination and structuring capability — transforms Tipolis from an issuer dependent on intermediaries into a platform that owns the full capital markets value chain.

### 6.2 The Technology Inflection

The timing of this strategic decision is critical, and the direction of travel is unambiguous: the cost of building financial infrastructure is falling dramatically and will continue to fall as artificial intelligence reduces the labor cost of compliance, legal drafting, investor communications, and regulatory reporting, and as blockchain technology reduces the settlement, custody, and record-keeping costs of securities issuance to near zero.

The white-label financial technology infrastructure required to issue, settle, and service tokenized securities — which includes City Bonds — is becoming accessible at a fraction of the cost it carried five years ago. Platforms that required a team of 50 compliance and operations specialists can now be operated by a team of 10 with AI-augmented workflows. Smart contract-based bond servicing on networks like Botanix or Liquid Network automates monthly distribution payments, covenant monitoring, and maturity events without manual intervention. Investor KYC and AML processes that previously required dedicated compliance teams can be executed by AI-powered identity verification systems at scale.

The window to build this infrastructure proprietary — before it becomes table stakes for all issuers and the differentiation advantage disappears — is open now and will begin to narrow. Tipolis that invests in this infrastructure today builds a capability that will be progressively cheaper to operate and progressively harder for competitors to replicate from scratch.

### 6.3 "The Channel Is the Asset, the Product Is the Commodity"

The clearest way to state this strategic logic is through a principle drawn from the most successful technology and financial businesses of the last three decades: the distribution channel is the asset; the product is the commodity.

Any organization with capital and legal expertise can issue a bond. Any organization with access to Bitcoin can create a hybrid instrument. Any city developer can present a project to investors. What cannot be replicated quickly is a trusted, established, direct relationship with 500 family offices, 100 credit funds, 50 infrastructure funds, and 20 sovereign wealth funds, accumulated through years of consistent execution and transparent communication. Tipolis's own financial infrastructure creates this distribution channel as a proprietary asset. Each issuance builds the channel. Each monthly distribution made on time reinforces the relationship. Each successful project delivery validates the investment thesis for the next round. The channel compounds in value in a way that the product — the specific terms of a bond — does not.

This is precisely what Strategy has built with STRF, STRD, and STRK: not merely three financial products, but a recurring, institutionally recognized access mechanism to capital markets where Strategy is the counterparty of choice for a specific investment thesis. Tipolis's ambition is to occupy that same position within the International City and private jurisdiction asset class.

## 8. Credit Rating Improvement Strategy

The central financial objective of the City Bond program — beyond raising capital — is the progressive improvement of the credit rating assigned to Tipolis's instruments by international rating agencies. A higher credit rating means a lower cost of capital, a larger investor universe, greater issuance volumes, and better terms. Every basis point of improvement in the weighted average cost of capital across the City Bond suite translates directly into better operational leverage for International City development.

The credit improvement strategy operates on four dimensions:

**Track record construction:** Each successful monthly distribution, each project delivery, and each bond maturity where principal and Bitcoin appreciation payments are delivered as promised incrementally improves Tipolis's credit profile. Rating agencies weight demonstrated repayment history heavily. The program must be designed from the outset to prioritize payment discipline above all else in the early issuances.

**Bitcoin reserve quality:** As the Bitcoin retained by Tipolis grows in value, the asset quality of its balance sheet improves, which is a straightforward credit positive. Rating agencies that have begun to incorporate digital asset reserves into their analytical frameworks — as they are beginning to do for Strategy's instruments — will reward this collateral quality improvement.

**Operational diversification:** A portfolio of International Cities across different host countries and geographies reduces concentration risk, which is a primary concern for any rating agency analyzing city-level revenue bonds. A multi-city program distributes those risks, making the aggregate performance more predictable and the monthly cash flow stream more stable.

**Structural improvement:** As the program matures, Tipolis can introduce additional credit enhancement mechanisms: reserve funds that hold 6 to 12 months of monthly distribution obligations in liquid assets, performance covenants that trigger investor protections in defined circumstances, third-party liquidity guarantees for the senior tranche, and co-investment by the host country government in a first-loss position that protects senior bondholders.

## 9. The Virtuous Cycle — Capital as a Compounding Strategic Asset

The most important long-term dynamic of the City Bond program is the compounding relationship between capital access and operational execution. These are not independent variables — they reinforce each other in a cycle that, once established, becomes self-accelerating.

Greater capital access enables larger and faster deployment of operational infrastructure. A city that builds faster reaches stabilized operational revenue sooner, reducing the ramp-period carry deficit and improving the near-term financial profile. Better operational performance generates stronger cash flows to service monthly bond obligations, improving coverage ratios and payment track records. A stronger track record and better coverage ratios improve Tipolis's credit rating. A better credit rating reduces the cost of capital for the next issuance. Lower cost of capital allows issuance at better terms — lower coupons, longer durations, larger volumes — which attracts a broader investor universe. A broader investor universe allows faster capital raises at scale. And faster capital raises at scale enable even larger operational deployments.

This cycle is the same one that has driven the compounding capital access advantages of the best infrastructure and real estate capital allocators over the past three decades. Brookfield Asset Management, Macquarie Infrastructure, and Prologis did not build their capital market positions through superior products — they built them through superior track records that compounded into superior access, which compounded into superior scale, which compounded into superior terms. Tipolis's City Bond program is designed to establish and accelerate this same cycle from the first issuance.

The Bitcoin component is an accelerant to this cycle. In scenarios where Bitcoin appreciates materially, Tipolis's balance sheet strengthens ahead of maturity dates, providing additional visible collateral that rating agencies and investors can observe. A publicly verifiable, appreciating asset on the corporate balance sheet — particularly in an era where on-chain verification is becoming standard — creates a transparency advantage that conventional infrastructure bonds cannot offer.

## 10. Summary — City Bond Program at a Glance

The City Bond program represents Tipolis's transition from a city developer that raises capital episodically through conventional channels into a capital markets platform that systematically funds International City development through programmatic issuance, proprietary distribution infrastructure, and Bitcoin-enhanced return structures.

Three instruments serve three investor segments: CB6-20y targets senior capital from sovereign funds, pension funds, and insurance companies with a 6% fixed coupon paid monthly, 20-year duration, and senior claim on city assets. The host country benefits from this infrastructure beyond its role as the ICT host: the same regulatory, technological, and investor relations capabilities can be applied to the government's own financing needs, creating a Bitcoin-enhanced sovereign or quasi-sovereign instrument that improves the government's fiscal position while cementing its alignment with the City Bond program's success.

## 11. STRC as a Liquidity Cushion — Monthly Coupon Coverage Analysis

### 11.1 The Role of STRC on Tipolis's Balance Sheet

One of the most important structural characteristics of the City Bond program is the 3-year construction ramp period, during which no operational revenue is generated by newly deployed capital while monthly distribution obligations to investors are already running. This ramp deficit is the primary near-term liquidity risk for Tipolis as an issuer — and the most consequential period for investor confidence.

Tipolis's approach to this risk draws from the same logic applied to its corporate treasury management: allocating a portion of its liquid corporate reserves into STRC (Strategy's preferred stock, listed on the NYSE under the ticker STRC). It is essential to clarify what this analysis is and what it is not: the STRC allocation is a separate, independent corporate treasury decision made with Tipolis's own liquid capital. It does not involve any portion of the funds raised through City Bonds. Capital raised by CB6-20y, CB10-10y, or CIP always follows the fixed 90/10 allocation rule — 90% to operational city assets, 10% to Bitcoin. None of that capital is redirected into STRC.

The analysis in this section answers a different and specific question: for each dollar that Tipolis allocates to STRC from its own corporate treasury, how much annual and monthly distribution obligation does the resulting income cover? This is a liquidity efficiency comparison — understanding how much STRC, held as a corporate liquidity buffer, is required to fully service each instrument's investor obligations independently of the deployed project capital.

STRC is a USD-denominated preferred equity instrument backed by Strategy's Bitcoin reserves — approximately $49 billion in BTC as of early 2026, providing 4.5× overcollateralization against the approximately $3.4 billion in outstanding STRC obligations. STRC is publicly traded at a par value of approximately $100 per share, with average daily trading volume exceeding $80 million, making it highly liquid by any institutional standard. STRC distributes between 9% and 12% per annum to holders in monthly USD cash payments — a distribution schedule that aligns precisely with Tipolis's monthly City Bond obligation structure.

For Tipolis, holding STRC on its corporate balance sheet serves three functions simultaneously: it generates high-yield monthly USD income that can be applied directly to bond distribution obligations, it provides a liquid asset that can be sold rapidly if additional cash is needed, and it can be pledged as institutional collateral for credit facilities, providing access to leverage without forced liquidation.

The analysis below models two scenarios: a base case at 11% annual STRC yield and a pessimistic case at 7% annual STRC yield. The 7% scenario represents a meaningful deterioration from historical STRC distribution rates and is used to stress-test the cushion's adequacy under adverse conditions. All figures are presented per $1,000,000 of City Bond face value outstanding.

---

### 11.2 Per-$1,000,000 Bond STRC Coverage — Annual and Monthly Obligations

The core metric is: for a given amount of STRC held by Tipolis as a corporate liquidity buffer, how much of each instrument's annual and monthly distribution obligation does the resulting STRC income cover?

**Monthly distribution obligations per $1,000,000 bond:**

| Instrument | Annual obligation | Monthly obligation |
|---|---|---|
| CB6-20y (6% coupon) | $60,000 | $5,000 |
| CB10-10y (10% coupon) | $100,000 | $8,333 |
| CIP floor (4% distribution) | $40,000 | $3,333 |

**STRC balance required for full annual obligation coverage — base case (11% yield):**

To fully cover each instrument's annual obligation from STRC monthly income alone, Tipolis must hold the following amount of STRC per $1,000,000 bond outstanding:

| Instrument | Annual obligation | Monthly obligation | STRC required at 11% | Monthly STRC income |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CB6-20y | $60,000 | $5,000 | $545,455 | $5,000 |
| CB10-10y | $100,000 | $8,333 | $909,091 | $8,333 |
| CIP floor | $40,000 | $3,333 | $363,636 | $3,333 |

The alignment here is structurally elegant: STRC pays monthly, City Bonds pay monthly. A Tipolis STRC position sized to 54.5% of CB6-20y face value ($545,455 per $1M bond) generates exactly $5,000 per month in STRC distributions — matching the CB6-20y monthly coupon obligation dollar for dollar, allowing Tipolis to service investor payments entirely from STRC income during the ramp period without touching any other cash reserves.

**STRC balance required for full annual obligation coverage — pessimistic case (7% yield):**

| Instrument | Annual obligation | Monthly obligation | STRC required at 7% | Monthly STRC income |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CB6-20y | $60,000 | $5,000 | $857,143 | $5,000 |
| CB10-10y | $100,000 | $8,333 | $1,428,571 | $8,333 |
| CIP floor | $40,000 | $3,333 | $571,429 | $3,333 |

**Key observation — pessimistic case:** At 7% STRC yield, covering the CB10-10y monthly obligation entirely from STRC income would require $1,428,571 in STRC — 42.9% more than the bond's face value. This confirms that CB10-10y's monthly distribution service during the ramp period must rely on a combination of STRC income and other corporate cash sources. For CB6-20y and CIP, the pessimistic STRC coverage is feasible at STRC positions of 85.7% and 57.1% of face value respectively — still within a reasonable liquidity buffer range for an institution managing an ongoing capital raise program.

### 11.3 Investor-Facing Interpretation

From the perspective of a CB6-20y investor — the most conservative and senior instrument in the suite — a Tipolis STRC allocation of $545,455 per $1,000,000 bond outstanding is sufficient to generate exactly $5,000 per month in STRC distributions, matching the CB6-20y monthly coupon obligation precisely at the base 11% STRC yield. This means that during the 3 years in which the city is being built and no operational revenue has yet arrived, every monthly payment owed to the investor can be funded exclusively from Tipolis's STRC distributions — without touching any operational cash, without asset sales, and without leverage.

Even in the stress scenario where STRC distributes only 7%, a STRC position equal to approximately 86% of CB6-20y face value ($857,143 per $1M bond) would still cover 100% of the monthly coupon obligation. The senior tranche's monthly obligations are effectively insulated from ramp-period cash flow risk under all realistic scenarios.

For CB10-10y, the higher monthly coupon ($8,333 per $1M bond) means that full coverage from STRC requires a position approaching or exceeding the bond's face value in adverse yield scenarios. This confirms the design intent: CB10-10y's negative carry is managed at the enterprise portfolio level — supplemented by CB6-20y positive carry and general corporate reserves — rather than fully hedged by STRC alone.

For CIP, the low floor monthly distribution of $3,333 per $1M bond makes STRC coverage highly capital-efficient. A STRC position of $363,636 (36.4% of face value) at 11% yield exactly matches the CIP floor obligation monthly. Even at 7% yield, a STRC position of $571,429 fully covers the CIP floor — making CIP the most resilient instrument from a ramp-period liquidity perspective.

The key strategic insight is this: STRC on Tipolis's corporate balance sheet is not a speculative position or a yield-enhancement trade. It is a structural monthly liquidity mechanism funded by Tipolis's own capital — entirely separate from bond proceeds — that directly addresses the most predictable and consequential risk in the City Bond program: the gap between the moment capital is raised and the moment operational revenue begins. Because STRC pays monthly and City Bonds pay monthly, the cash flow matching is exact and automatic. The instrument is liquid enough to be sold at any point if conditions require, and stable enough — backed by 4.5× Bitcoin overcollateralization — to serve as institutional collateral for credit facilities. For Tipolis, STRC is the liquidity infrastructure that makes City Bond monthly distribution obligations credible to institutional investors from day one.
